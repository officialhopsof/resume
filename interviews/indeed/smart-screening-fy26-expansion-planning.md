# 1:00 PM – 1:45 PM | Integrations (Disposition Sync, Candidate Lifecycle & CRAVE)
· Leads: Katie Aretz (for Divya Agarwal) / Hannah Ostrander / Marcel Plante / Vishwajeet Chhikara
· Goal: Align on how candidate status lifecycle, outbound disposition sync, and CRAVE intersect for Smart Screening — and what needs to be built across teams in FY26
· Context: The jobseeker journey is changing with the introduction of the CRAVE workflow in the Apply. With several systems tracking candidate status, we need to align on which statuses are assigned to jobseekers at various steps in the workflow.
1. CandStat & Candidate Lifecycle in UCP: CandStat is Indeed's centralized gRPC service for managing candidate submission statuses — the single source of truth for where a candidate is in the hiring process. As Smart Screening moves to UCP (via Nexus Scaling), candidate lifecycle management must be well-defined.
a. The Candidate Submission Employer Status List doc captures the current unified status set across submission source types (RESUME_SEARCH, INDEED_APPLY, ATS_APPLICATION), including: New, Reviewing, Screening, Interviewing, Offer Made, Hired, Not Selected, and others.
b. Smart Screening specifically cares about the "Rejected" / "Not Selected" status for automations and ROI measurement
2. Jobseeker pause and re-entry for AI recruiter is also creating a new candidates status of “PAUSED”
3. Project CRAVE (Compliant Verifications) is Indeed's initiative to enable compliant license/certification verification during Smart Screening. This introduces new candidate lifecycle states:
a. PENDING_VERIFICATION — Waiting for verification results (24-48 hours). Candidate not yet delivered to ATS.
b. PENDING_ALTERNATE_EVIDENCE — Invalid result, only viewed by jobseeker and they are allowed to dispute results
c. VERIFIED_VALID — License verified, all employer actions enabled, candidate sent to ATS.
4. Interop Disposition Sync (ATS ↔ Indeed):
a. Outbound (Indeed → ATS): In the Smart Screening backlog as a P3 item — described as "HIGH priority for dependency teams to implement their parts, but will not be ready for customer-facing delivery until April or later." Requires solving complex problems: mapping Indeed statuses back to ATS-specific statuses, handling rejection emails/notes/reasons, and ATS workflow validations.
b. Current gap: Smart Screening automation candidate outcomes (e.g. employer messages a candidate based on score threshold, etc..) are NOT delivered back to the ATS — there is no indication in the ATS to the employer that anything happened to the candidate on Indeed.
· Why this matters for scaling Smart Screening:
· Our ability to deploy automations based on a candidate’s status while providing a great candidate experience
· Calculate ROI based on hiring results
· Enterprise clients primarily manage candidates in their ATS — they need to be aware of on-Indeed disposition changes reflected in their system of record
· The current known limitation ("Cannot review candidates marked as 'Declined' for other roles — no ATS status sync") is called out in Smart Screening's own product documentation as a gap

# Session Recap
Aly Baxter presented a comprehensive overview of the state of smart screening, reflecting on how much the product has evolved in under a year. The session covered current traction (600+ contracts, $5M+ committed revenue, 700+ adopting parents), the top drivers and objections from employers, features that are resonating, key bottlenecks around job enrollment and manual opt-ins, and major opportunities ahead including auto-enrollment, verification expansion, automated rejection, and fraud/risk flagging.
# Key Takeaways
· Over 600 signed contracts and $5M+ in committed revenue; 90% of early buyers purchased without ever using the product, based purely on the problem resonating.
· The candidate summary page is a standout value add — employers love the explainability, scoring, and recent experience visibility.
· A data-approved claim of ~20% time-to-hire reduction is strongly resonating in sales conversations.
· A free trial test was launched for 500+ mid-market employers; success threshold is 15%+ conversion to paying.
· Rules feature is popular but has limitations (e.g., can't set AI recruiter questions via rules); employers think in "job profiles" and want rules to cover everything.
· Job enrollment is stagnant at ~11% of eligible jobs; license/certification verification adoption is at ~18%. Both need to increase significantly.
· Top 100 enterprise initiative is underway; many large employers won't adopt Indeed Apply but want fraud protection and screening for all candidates, not just Indeed candidates.
· Fraud and bot detection are rapidly growing concerns — Gartner predicts 1 in 4 applications will be fake by 2030; employers report fraudulent applications expected to grow 1,000%+ in 2026.
· License/certification upload feature is unexpectedly popular, with 4,000+ supported certifications. Driver's license verification is highly requested but blocked by PII constraints.
· Talent rediscovery / sourcing on employer's own talent pools is an interesting opportunity that doesn't require Indeed Apply, since it uses bidirectional candidate sync.
# Decisions Made
· Auto-enrollment is the top priority to unblock all downstream value; everything is gated by the inability to increase job enrollment beyond ~11%.
· Automation is the "most valuable feature" and will be used as the metric for time-to-value tracking.
· Elimination of unnecessary manual opt-ins and user agreements is a priority to reduce employer friction.
· Verification (license/certification upload) expansion will be brought back in a compliant manner, with employers making clear they want to filter out unqualified candidates pre-application.
· Automated rejection will be built so employers can close the loop with candidates they won't pursue.
· Risk flagging and fraud detection will be invested in given the growing market demand.
· Pricing for talent rediscovery will charge for the act of finding candidates, even from the employer's own pool — analogous to how LLMs charge for querying your own data.

# Session Recap
This session explored the challenge and opportunity of screening non-Indeed candidates — a key requirement for penetrating large enterprise employers (Fortune 100) who don't use Indeed Apply and want screening across 100% of their applicants regardless of source. Aly Baxter framed the employer perspective (compliance, brand consistency, top-of-funnel filtering), and the group brainstormed technical approaches including an API contract (via Inbound CandidateSync), white-labeled Indeed Apply, ad hoc AI Recruiter triggering, and modular workflow steps. The discussion also covered score confidence, data parity challenges, and legal requirements for non-Indeed job seekers.
# Key Takeaways
· Large employers like Salesforce, Walmart, Visa, and Barclays won't adopt a screening solution that only works for a subset of their candidates — it's a compliance and brand consistency non-starter.
· Three main employer objections to partial screening: (1) scoring only some candidates isn't compliant, (2) inconsistent job seeker experiences hurt their brand, (3) it creates more work, not less.
· The core technical insight: the bare minimum for scoring is a job description and a resume — AI Recruiter, verification, and screener questions are all optional enrichment.
· Two architectural approaches discussed: (1) Define a strict API contract where the employer/partner provides all required data, or (2) Accept whatever data is available and have Indeed trigger workflows to gather the rest.
· Inbound CandidateSync (ICS) is the scalable integration path — already live with Google/iSoft, built (not on) for Workday. It creates UCP candidate submissions that Nexus can trigger off of.
· A "one-shot" scoring API (give job + candidate, get score back, forget about it) was proposed as a low-fidelity entry point for testing and upselling.
· Framing matters: employers respond better to "job seeker enrichment" and "powering the backend of your apply" than to "screening" or "filtering."
· The concept of a confidence score alongside the 0-100 score was discussed to address varying data completeness — FY26 exploration item.
· Legal challenge: non-Indeed candidates may need to create Indeed accounts to sign TOS before Indeed can score them, though there's precedent (CMI third-party ATS plugin).
· Job seeker data reuse across employers could reduce friction over time — once a job seeker creates an Indeed account and builds a profile, subsequent screenings are faster.
# Decisions Made
· The non-Indeed candidate scoring capability is not a formal roadmap commitment yet, but it will be prioritized as Fortune 100 deals demand it.
· ICS (Inbound CandidateSync) will be the primary scalable integration path for non-Indeed candidate scoring.
· The initial approach should be: import candidate, score via Nexus, send results back — no other system touches these candidates initially (simplifies legal scope).
· Employers are assumed to not come to Indeed for candidate management in this use case — they are purely indexed, operating entirely in their ATS.
· A logged-in-first approach for job seekers is preferred; logged-out support can be explored if friction proves to be an issue.
· Both paths must be pursued in parallel: ATS partnership integrations (long tail, 3-4 years) and direct API/white-label solutions (near-term).

# Session Recap
Steve presented the Nexus Scaling architecture, which replaces the dependency on TPIA on Indeed (Third-Party Integration Architecture on Indeed) with a new job ownership service. · The goal is not to eliminate TPIA's behavior (managing candidates on Indeed) but to deprecate the extra configuration steps required to enable it.
· If advertiser assignment is wrong, candidates end up in a "black hole" — there is currently no way to reassign them once created in UCP.
· Three long-term architecture options: (1) push all functionality to ATS via APIs so employers never return to Indeed, (2) continue improving the job ownership service, (3) change UCP to not require advertiser ID and use SpiceDB for flexible permissions.

· Premium Plus just launched and depends on TPIA on Indeed for getting indexed candidates into TME — there's currently no other way.
· A UCP-to-TME shim layer could provide a degraded but functional experience for indexed candidates in TME without requiring TPIA on Indeed.
· TME feature gaps for UCP candidates include: notes, status/disposition sync, sentiment, and attributes.
· The "employer job" concept is being developed as a flexible, long-lived job type to replace the pattern of closing and reposting jobs.
· The advertiser concept at Indeed is overloaded — it serves as billing entity, permissions scope, and organizational unit simultaneously, which creates cascading complexity.
· S&C (Sourcing & Candidates) team is working on unifying the sourcing and candidate management pipeline, which shares the same need for TME to support UCP candidates.
# Decisions Made
· Nexus Scaling will proceed on schedule; a few advertisers will be fully running on the new systems by end of March, with Canadian pilot advertisers added in early April.
· The job ownership service is the interim ("band-aid") solution for advertiser-job association — it's adequate for now but won't scale indefinitely.
· TPIA on Indeed configuration will eventually be deprecated, but it's no longer a screening-team-driven impetus — it's a simplification effort.
· Premium Plus will continue using TPIA on Indeed for now, until an alternative path for indexed candidates in TME is available.
· Taylor and the Nexus product team need to take a position on the timeline and priority for hybrid customer support before further architecture decisions can be made.
· A degraded TME experience for indexed candidates (view, sort, rank — without full feature parity) is an acceptable initial step.

# Session Recap
Marcel led a session on what structured data and signals need to flow between Nexus, UCP, Interop, and ATSs as smart screening scales. The group worked through the auto-reject workflow for license verification (when a candidate admits they lack a required license), the distinction between auto-reject and auto-filter, and whether Interop needs changes to support these flows. A significant portion of the conversation focused on status management: distinguishing Nexus workflow statuses from UCP candidate/disposition statuses, whether to unify them across surfaces (Nexus Hub, TME), and how to avoid conflicting statuses. The session concluded with alignment that Interop has no short-term changes needed for verification/auto-reject, and that Nexus, UCP, and TME teams need to coordinate on status terminology and reconciliation.
# Key Takeaways
· Auto-reject is a Nexus automation, not an ATS delivery concern — if a candidate says they don't have a required license, Nexus signals Interop "don't deliver" and the employer never receives that application in their ATS.
· License verification is a post-apply workflow: the job seeker submits the application, Nexus triggers third-party verification afterward. If it fails, the job seeker is notified to upload alternative evidence. Only one re-verification attempt is allowed to prevent abuse of per-transaction vendor costs.
· Indeed is NOT following the FCRA process — they are using a compliance-adjacent approach where the job seeker self-attests and provides alternative evidence, rather than a formal dispute workflow.
· Interop has no immediate changes needed for the verification/auto-reject flow — the existing hold (up to 6 months) and release signals already accommodate the certification workflow timeline.
· For MVP, applications will always be delivered regardless of license verification outcome. The only scenario blocking delivery is when an employer's rules say "don't send candidates without a license" and the candidate answers "no."
· Status syncing from Indeed to ATS (e.g., "interviewing" after an on-Indeed automation) is a near-term business need — employers won't adopt on-Indeed automations unless results are reflected in their ATS. This requires the OCS API and partner adoption.
· "Nexus status" is an overloaded term — the group identified three distinct types: workflow status (orchestration state within Nexus), disposition status (lifecycle stage like rejected/hired in UCP), and delivery status (whether the application has been forwarded to the ATS).
· UCP owns candidate disposition statuses; Nexus owns workflow statuses. There should be one unified status system across Indeed — not product-specific statuses per surface.
· UCP currently dual-writes status from DRADIS and has status endpoints available, but TME does not yet consume them.
· The bad actor flag from Trust and Safety is being integrated into UCP (Rahul working with Vishrajit on intake).
# Decisions Made
· Interop has no short-term changes needed to support license verification or auto-reject — existing hold/release signals are sufficient.
· Auto-reject and auto-filter are distinct concepts: auto-reject is an employer-configured automation that marks a candidate as rejected (but may still deliver); auto-filter prevents ATS delivery entirely.
· A rejected status is a candidate disposition status (owned by UCP), not a workflow status — UCP is the right place to store it.
· Status should not be product-specific — there should be one unified candidate status system across Indeed (via UCP), regardless of which surface (Nexus Hub, TME) displays it.
· For MVP, application delivery will never be blocked by a license verification failure — only by the candidate explicitly admitting they lack a required license.
· Interop will hold applications for up to 6 months (matching Indeed Apply's retention) if Nexus sends a "hold indefinitely" signal; after that, the application goes to cold storage.
· Status syncing from Indeed to ATS is confirmed as a near-term need and will require the OCS API with partner-by-partner adoption.
· The workflow orchestrator (not Interop, not UCP) is responsible for managing workflow state and stamping the appropriate lifecycle status on the UCP candidate when the workflow reaches a terminal state.
